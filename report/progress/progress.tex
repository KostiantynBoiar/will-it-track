\noindent
This report explains, in plain terms and with pictures, \emph{how} each part of the system works and what
is built so far. The one-line goal: given a new animal in a new place, \textbf{predict how well SAM\,3 will
track it \emph{before} we run it}, and explain \emph{why} --- separately for \textbf{finding} the animal
(\code{pDetA}) and \textbf{following} it (\code{pAssA}).

\section{Step 1 --- Getting the data}
Everything is built on \textbf{SA-FARI}: thousands of short camera-trap videos of wild animals, each frame
hand-outlined. Four lists describe it, linked by ids (Fig.~\ref{fig:schema}):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{videos} --- one entry per clip, with its frame files, its camera \emph{location}, and \emph{when}
        it was filmed.
  \item \textbf{categories} --- a big dictionary of text concepts (``a monkey'', ``ocelot'', \dots), each with
        a \code{category\_id}. The real animals carry a full \emph{tree-of-life} classification; most entries
        are generic distractors with no classification.
  \item \textbf{annotations} --- the ground-truth outlines (masks), one per frame, for the animals that are
        actually present.
  \item \textbf{probes} (\code{video\_np\_pairs}) --- the questions we ask: ``is \emph{this animal} in
        \emph{this video}?''. Many are \textbf{hard negatives} --- the animal is \emph{absent}, and a good
        model should return nothing.
\end{itemize}
We fetch the outlines from Hugging Face (a gated download) and pull only the frames we need from a public
Google bucket, on demand. A key early correction: the animal's identity is the \code{category\_id}, not the
raw prompt text (two different prompts can mean the same species), so everything keys on \code{category\_id}.

\section{Step 2 --- The two experiments}
The whole idea rests on a \emph{reference} (``what we've seen'') and a \emph{probe} (``the new thing''): a
distance measures how far a probe is from the reference. Inspecting the data changed our plan here. The
official split holds out \emph{new places} but re-uses the \emph{same species} on both sides --- so on that
split ``species novelty'' is always zero. Because SAM\,3 is \textbf{frozen} (it never trains on any of this),
the split is only \emph{our} choice of reference, so we are free to re-slice it. We therefore run \textbf{two
complementary experiments} (Fig.~\ref{fig:splits}):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Species hold-out} (primary). Hide one species at a time and ask ``how novel is it versus
        \emph{all the others}?'' --- \emph{leave-one-species-out}. This is the detection/novelty test.
  \item \textbf{Location hold-out} (the official split). Reference $=$ one region, probe $=$ a totally
        different region. This is the environment/association test.
\end{itemize}
The \emph{leakage firewall} is simply a closed-book rule: when we score how novel species $X$ is, $X$ is
never allowed into its own reference --- otherwise it would trivially look ``distance $0$ from itself''. A
test checks this.

\section{Step 3 --- The four ``how new is it?'' distances}
Each distance answers a plain question and is computed with \emph{no label of the target animal}.

\subsection*{Taxonomic --- how far apart on the tree of life?}
Count the steps up the family tree until two animals share an ancestor: same genus $=1$, same family $=2$,
same order $=3$, and so on (Fig.~\ref{fig:tree}). For each species we take the distance to its
\emph{nearest other} species. Across the $72$ fully-classified species this spreads from $0$ to $4$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:taxhist}) --- most animals have a same-family neighbour, but a fifth are clearly novel
($\ge 3$). The two $0$s are a quirk: ``pig'' and ``wild boar'' are the \emph{same} species under two names.
This spread is what makes the novelty axis testable.

\subsection*{Temporal --- how many years apart?}
The simplest one: the number of years between the probe's footage and the nearest reference footage
(2014\,$\rule[0.4ex]{5mm}{0.6pt}$\,2024). A 2023 clip against a 2015 reference is a gap of $8$.

\subsection*{Visual --- how unfamiliar does the animal \emph{look}?}
We cut the animal out of a few frames using its outline (background blacked out, so we measure the
\emph{animal}, not the bush behind it), pass each cut-out through a frozen vision model (DINOv2) to get a
numeric ``fingerprint'', and average a species' fingerprints into one \textbf{prototype}. The distance is
how different a species' prototype is from the nearest \emph{other} prototype (cosine distance;
Fig.~\ref{fig:vispipe}). On a six-species check the spread is sensible --- the pig looks most distinct, the
smaller mammals cluster together (Fig.~\ref{fig:visbar}).

\subsection*{Environment --- how unfamiliar does the \emph{place} look?}
Same trick for the scene: \emph{erase} the animal (fill its pixels with the average background) so only the
habitat remains, fingerprint that, and average per camera site into a ``typical scene''. The distance is
how far a probe site looks from the nearest reference site. A bonus flag spots \textbf{night / infrared}
footage by noticing the frame is basically grey. On the location hold-out the distances ran roughly
$0.06$--$0.44$, and the infrared night sites were flagged correctly while the daytime colour sites were not.

\section{Step 4 --- Measuring SAM\,3 (the scores we predict)}
This is the ``truth'' we compare against. \textbf{SAM\,3} is a \emph{promptable} tracker: give it a text
prompt (``ocelot'') and it finds and follows every matching animal across the video --- with no training on
that species. The official evaluator splits its score into the two things we care about (Fig.~\ref{fig:meas}):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \code{pDetA} --- \textbf{detection}: did it \emph{find} the animals (and not hallucinate on the hard
        negatives)?
  \item \code{pAssA} --- \textbf{association}: did it keep \emph{each identity} consistent over time?
\end{itemize}
Our harness runs this over every probe and writes one small file per video, so a run can stop and resume
without losing work (important on Colab, where sessions time out). The evaluator then turns the outlines
into the two numbers per cell. \emph{This code is built and tested with a stand-in; the real run happens on
a GPU.}

\section{Step 5 --- Fitting the law (planned)}
With the distances ($X$) and scores ($Y$) in hand, the last stage fits a small, honest model:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A curve, not a black box.} A beta regression (a straight line through a ``squashing''
        transform, because scores live in $[0,1]$) maps distances to a predicted score --- one model for
        detection, one for association.
  \item \textbf{Which factor matters?} Variance partitioning asks whether \emph{detection} is driven by
        \emph{species novelty} and \emph{association} by \emph{environment} --- the headline contrast.
  \item \textbf{Does it generalise?} We hide whole species (and whole places), predict them from distance
        \emph{alone}, and check the prediction lands near the truth, with honest error bars
        (Fig.~\ref{fig:predline}). Success is a \emph{validated} predictor, not a higher score.
  \item \textbf{The tool.} Four distances in $\to$ predicted \code{pDetA}/\code{pAssA} $+$ a confidence
        interval out: the practical ``will it work here?'' answer.
\end{itemize}

\section{Where we are, and the next step}
The \textbf{distance side is finished and sanity-checked} on real data, and the \textbf{measurement side is
built}. The codebase is typed, configuration-driven, lint-clean, and covered by a growing test suite; the
label-free features were refactored and verified to reproduce their outputs exactly. The immediate milestone
is the \textbf{first decision gate}: run frozen SAM\,3 over the test split on Colab (weights access is
confirmed), score it, and check the numbers sit in SA-FARI's published SAM\,3 range. Passing that gate opens
the modelling half --- fitting the detection-versus-association law and building the reliability estimator.
